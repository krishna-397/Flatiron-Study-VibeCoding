Plate 04 of my spatial canvases argued that the Flatiron exists because a diagonal crossed a grid. This map tests that claim against real coordinates. Broadway — a Lenape trail that predates the 1811 plan and refuses it — cuts north-west across Manhattan, and at every avenue it crosses it produces the same figure: a bowtie of two acute triangles. One is usually kept open as a square; the other is usually built on as a sliver. The Flatiron and Madison Square Park are one such pair. Data is authored, and every feature carries a source field — a practice inherited from my precedent study, where provenance is a column in the data rather than a footnote.

GEOSPATIAL STRUCTURES IN MY OWN WORK

This is the digital object closest to my capstone. My research is on Indian farmlands — how models of farming are outdating faster than the forces acting on them — and that is a fundamentally geospatial problem: a farm is a parcel, and a parcel is defined by its boundary, its neighbours, its water, and its distance to a market. The same structure I use here to show a diagonal producing leftover triangles is the structure I need to show fragmentation of holdings across generations, catchment areas that ignore property lines, and the gap between where crops are grown and where they are sold. Two inheritances carry directly across: provenance as a field, so that a satellite-derived boundary and a surveyed one never read as equally certain in a dataset where much is missing; and the square/sliver pair as an analytic figure — the habit of looking for what a system produces as a by-product, and asking who gets to use it. The next step is layers that are not just legible but comparable: scenarios a community could look at together and argue about.
